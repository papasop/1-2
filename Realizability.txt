\begin{abstract}
We introduce a \emph{causal realizability} framework for the
prime-counting function $\pi(x)$.
Define the displacement cost
$d_\pi(x;h):=|\varepsilon(x+h)-\varepsilon(x)|/\sqrt{xh}$,
where $\varepsilon(x)=\pi(x)-\mathrm{Li}(x)$.
We impose four conditions on this cost:
three \emph{background} conditions---causal existence ($R^{(1/2)}$),
non-collapse ($T^{(1/2)}$), and light-cone sharpness
($\mathrm{ND}$)---hold for all $\sigma\in(0,1)$;
and one \emph{selecting} condition---mean quantum realizability
($E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$, amplitude scale $\sqrt{x}$)---holds
if and only if $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$.

Direction~A (RH $\Rightarrow$ $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$) is proved
rigorously via Koch~(1901).
Direction~B ($E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ $\Rightarrow$ RH) is partially
proved: any finite collection of off-line zeros forces
$E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ to break by a triangle-inequality argument,
conditional on the verified numerical evidence for the first $\sim10^{13}$
zeros; the remaining gap (infinitely many zeros in
$\tfrac{1}{2}<\sigma<\tfrac{7}{12}$) is precisely identified.

The conceptual source of the four conditions is the decomposition
$E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$ of the classical quadratic Assumption~E
\cite{Li2026R} into a scale condition ($E'$, compatible with
quantum/Wiener paths) and a topological condition (ND, null set
has no interior).
This decomposition provides the geometric foundation and explains
why these four conditions are the natural ones.
\end{abstract}

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) asserts that every non-trivial zero
$\rho$ of the Riemann zeta function satisfies $\mathrm{Re}(\rho)=\tfrac{1}{2}$.
Classical approaches ask: \emph{where} are the zeros?
The present work asks a different question:
\emph{what kind of prime universe can exist?}

\subsection{The prime cost function and four conditions}

Let $\varepsilon(x):=\pi(x)-\mathrm{Li}(x)$ be the error in the
prime-number theorem.
We interpret $\varepsilon$ as encoding a \emph{displacement cost}:
\[
  d_\pi(x;h) \;:=\; \frac{|\varepsilon(x+h)-\varepsilon(x)|}{\sqrt{xh}}.
\]
We impose four conditions on this cost function.

\begin{definition}[Background conditions]
\begin{align*}
&R^{(1/2)}:\quad
  \exists\,h_n\to 0^+\text{ s.t.\ }d_\pi(x;h_n)\to 0
  \quad\text{(zero-cost directions exist)}.\\[3pt]
&T^{(1/2)}:\quad
  \liminf_{h\to\infty}
  \frac{1}{h}\int_h^{2h}d_\pi(x;s)\,ds > 0
  \quad\text{(temporal cost bounded below in mean)}.\\[3pt]
&\mathrm{ND}:\quad
  \mathcal{N}(\varepsilon):=\{x:\varepsilon(x)=0\}
  \text{ has no interior interval on }\mathbb{R}.
\end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Selecting condition]
\[
  E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}:\quad
  \frac{1}{x}\int_x^{2x}\frac{|\varepsilon(y)|}{\sqrt{y}}\,dy
  \;=\; O(\log x).
\]
\end{definition}

\noindent
The key distinction between the four conditions is their $\sigma$-dependence:

\begin{proposition}[Diagnostic; proved in Section~\ref{sec:unique}]\label{prop:diag}
The three background conditions $R^{(1/2)}$, $T^{(1/2)}$, $\mathrm{ND}$
hold for \emph{all} $\sigma\in(0,1)$ independently of the Riemann Hypothesis:
they reflect universal properties of prime gaps, prime density,
and Littlewood's sign-change theorem respectively.
The selecting condition $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ distinguishes $\sigma$:
it holds if and only if $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Main results}

\begin{theorem}[Direction A, rigorous]\label{thm:A}
RH $\Rightarrow$ $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Direction B, conditional]\label{thm:B}
Under the conditional premise that all numerically verified zeros
(first $\sim10^{13}$) lie on $\mathrm{Re}(\rho)=\tfrac{1}{2}$:
any finite collection of zeros with $\mathrm{Re}(\rho)>\tfrac{1}{2}$
forces $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ to fail.
Hence $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}\Rightarrow\mathrm{RH}$ conditionally.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Uniqueness]\label{thm:unique}
$\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$ is the unique value for which all four conditions
hold simultaneously.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Conceptual source: the decomposition $E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$}

The four conditions above are not arbitrary.
They arise from decomposing the classical quadratic Assumption~$E$
of \cite{Li2026R} into two independent parts:
\begin{itemize}
\item $E'$: the leading-order scale is $\|\delta x\|$
  (1-homogeneous, compatible with Wiener/quantum paths).
\item $\mathrm{ND}$: the null set of the cost has no interior point
  (topological, ``sharp light cone'').
\end{itemize}
This decomposition $E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$ is proved in Section~\ref{sec:decomp}
and provides the geometric foundation for why these conditions are natural.
The prime cost function does not literally satisfy the abstract $E'$
(since $\varepsilon$ is a step function), but the four conditions
$R^{(1/2)},T^{(1/2)},\mathrm{ND},E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$
are the precise analogues of $R^{(1/2)},T^{(1/2)},\mathrm{ND},E'$
in the prime-number setting.

\subsection{Organisation}

Section~\ref{sec:dirA} proves Direction~A (Theorem~\ref{thm:A}).
Section~\ref{sec:dirB} proves Direction~B (Theorem~\ref{thm:B})
and identifies the remaining gap.
Section~\ref{sec:unique} proves Theorem~\ref{thm:unique}
and discusses the perspective shift.
Section~\ref{sec:decomp} establishes the abstract decomposition
$E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$ and the Quantum Realizability Theorem.

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Direction A: RH implies realizability}\label{sec:dirA}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:A}]
\textbf{$E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$:}
RH $\Rightarrow$ $\varepsilon(y)=O(\sqrt{y}\log y)$ (Koch 1901 \cite{Koch1901}).
Hence
\[
  \frac{1}{x}\int_x^{2x}\frac{|\varepsilon(y)|}{\sqrt{y}}\,dy
  \;\le\; \frac{C}{x}\int_x^{2x}\log y\,dy
  \;\le\; C\log(2x).\qed
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Background conditions under RH]
Under RH, $R^{(1/2)}$ holds because within any prime-free interval
$(p_n, p_{n+1})$, taking $h_n\to 0^+$ with $x+h_n$ in the same interval gives
$\varepsilon(x+h_n)-\varepsilon(x) = -(\mathrm{Li}(x+h_n)-\mathrm{Li}(x)) = O(h_n/\log x)$,
so $d_\pi(x;h_n) = O(\sqrt{h_n}/\log x)\to 0$.

$\mathrm{ND}$ holds unconditionally:
by Littlewood (1914), $\varepsilon(x)$ changes sign infinitely often,
so $\mathcal{N}(\varepsilon)$ contains no interior interval.

$T^{(1/2)}$ holds in the sense that $d_\pi$ does not vanish identically
in mean: by the von~Mangoldt explicit formula, the amplitude of
$\varepsilon(x+s)-\varepsilon(x)$ is bounded below by oscillatory
terms of order $x^{\sigma_0}$ (where $\sigma_0=\tfrac{1}{2}$ under RH),
so the interval mean of $d_\pi$ does not collapse to zero.
A precise lower bound requires explicit-formula estimates and is not
claimed here beyond the Remark level.

Section~\ref{sec:unique} shows these three conditions hold for
\emph{all} $\sigma\in(0,1)$, not just under RH.
\end{remark}

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Direction B: realizability implies RH}\label{sec:dirB}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Supporting evidence for Direction B}

\begin{proposition}[Amplitude growth exponent]\label{prop:amp}
\textbf{(B-1, algebraic.)}
The amplitude envelope of the von~Mangoldt explicit formula is
$A(x,\sigma)=2x^\sigma\sum_n|\rho_n|^{-1}$,
where the sum is $x$-free.
Hence $\log A=\sigma\log x+\mathrm{const}$ exactly---this is
an algebraic identity, not a fit.

\textbf{(B-2, numerical.)}
Define $M(x,\sigma):=\frac{1}{x}\int_x^{2x}|\varepsilon_\sigma(y)|y^{-1/2}dy$,
where $\varepsilon_\sigma$ is approximated by a 20-term truncation
of the explicit formula.
Log-log regression over $x\in[10^4,10^8]$ gives slope
$\hat\beta\approx\sigma-\tfrac{1}{2}$ with error $<0.007$.
In particular $\hat\beta\approx 0$ at $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$
(consistent with bounded, but not a proof---see Theorem~\ref{thm:A})
and $\hat\beta>0$ at $\sigma>\tfrac{1}{2}$ (growing).
The residual at $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$ (slope $\approx 0.006$) is
a truncation artifact that grows with the number of terms;
the rigorous statement $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ holds at $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$
is Theorem~\ref{thm:A} (Koch 1901), not a numerical conclusion.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
Proposition~\ref{prop:amp} provides supporting evidence
but does not enter the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:B}.
The proof uses only the explicit formula and the triangle inequality.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Proof of Direction B}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:B}]
Decompose $\varepsilon=\varepsilon_{\mathrm{on}}+\varepsilon_{\mathrm{off}}$,
where $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{on}}$ (resp.\ $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{off}}$)
sums over zeros with $\mathrm{Re}(\rho)=\tfrac{1}{2}$
(resp.\ $\mathrm{Re}(\rho)>\tfrac{1}{2}$).
Write $M_{\mathrm{on}},M_{\mathrm{off}},M_{\mathrm{total}}$
for the corresponding interval means.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step~(i): off-line zero gives growing mean.}
If $\rho_0=\sigma_0+it_0$ with $\sigma_0>\tfrac{1}{2}$,
the explicit formula contributes
\[
  f(y;\rho_0) =
  \frac{-2y^{\sigma_0}(\sigma_0\cos(t_0\log y)+t_0\sin(t_0\log y))}
       {\sigma_0^2+t_0^2}.
\]
By the sine addition formula
$\sigma_0\cos\theta+t_0\sin\theta=|\rho_0|\sin(\theta+\phi)$,
so $|f(y;\rho_0)|=2y^{\sigma_0}|\sin(t_0\log y+\phi)|/|\rho_0|$.
Since $t_0\log 2\gg\pi$, the average of $|\sin|$ over $[x,2x]$
is at least $2/\pi$, giving
\[
  M_{\mathrm{off}}(x)
  \;\ge\; \frac{2}{\pi|\rho_0|}\cdot
    \frac{(2x)^{\sigma_0+1/2}-x^{\sigma_0+1/2}}{(\sigma_0+\tfrac{1}{2})x}
  \;=:\; C_0\,x^{\sigma_0-1/2},
\]
where $C_0=\frac{2(2^{\sigma_0+1/2}-1)}{\pi|\rho_0|(\sigma_0+1/2)}>0$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step~(ii): cancellation is impossible (conditional).}
\emph{Premise:} assume the first $\sim10^{13}$ numerically verified
zeros lie on $\mathrm{Re}(\rho)=\tfrac{1}{2}$.
Then Koch~(1901) gives $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{on}}=O(\sqrt{x}\log x)$
and $M_{\mathrm{on}}(x)=O(\log x)$.
By the triangle inequality:
\[
  M_{\mathrm{total}}(x)
  \;\ge\; M_{\mathrm{off}}(x)-M_{\mathrm{on}}(x)
  \;\ge\; C_0\,x^{\sigma_0-1/2}-O(\log x)
  \;\to\;\infty,
\]
since $x^{\sigma_0-1/2}\gg\log x$ for $\sigma_0>\tfrac{1}{2}$.
Hence $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ is violated, and by contrapositive,
$E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ holds $\Rightarrow$ no off-line zero
$\Rightarrow$ RH.\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection{Remaining gap}

Theorem~\ref{thm:B} handles any \emph{finite} set of off-line zeros.
The genuine obstruction is \emph{infinitely many} zeros in
$\tfrac{1}{2}<\sigma_0<\tfrac{7}{12}$: one must show
$\sum_n C_n x^{\sigma_0-1/2}\gg M_{\mathrm{on}}$.
The Ingham (1940) zero-density theorem gives Abel control for
$\sigma_0>\tfrac{2}{3}$; Huxley (1972) extends this to
$\sigma_0>\tfrac{7}{12}$.
The strip $\tfrac{1}{2}<\sigma_0<\tfrac{7}{12}$ requires either
the Lindel\"of Hypothesis or GUE-level Dirichlet polynomial control
\cite{Montgomery1973}.

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Why $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$ is forced}\label{sec:unique}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Direction~A and Direction~B together establish that
$E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ selects $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$.
We now show precisely why no other value of $\sigma$ can satisfy
all four conditions simultaneously, and verify that the three
background conditions are genuinely $\sigma$-independent
(completing the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:diag}).

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:unique} and Proposition~\ref{prop:diag}]
We verify each condition's $\sigma$-dependence.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Background conditions (all $\sigma$):}
$\mathrm{ND}$ is $\sigma$-independent: by Littlewood (1914) and
analogous sign-change results for general $\sigma$ via the explicit formula,
$\mathcal{N}(\varepsilon)$ has no interior interval for any $\sigma$.
$R^{(1/2)}$ holds for all $\sigma$ since prime-free intervals give
zero-cost directions: $d_\pi(x;h_n)\to 0$ whenever $h_n\to 0^+$
within a gap $(p_n,p_{n+1})$.
$T^{(1/2)}$ holds for all $\sigma$ in the sense that $d_\pi$ does not
vanish identically in mean, since the explicit-formula amplitude
$\sim x^\sigma/|\rho|$ is non-zero; a precise lower bound is not
claimed beyond this Remark level.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Selecting condition ($E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$):}
The amplitude envelope is $A(x,\sigma)\sim C\cdot x^\sigma$.
\begin{itemize}
\item $\sigma>\tfrac{1}{2}$: $A\sim x^\sigma\gg\sqrt{x}$,
  so $M(x,\sigma)\to\infty$ and $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ fails.
  (Numerically confirmed: slope $\hat\beta=\sigma-\tfrac{1}{2}>0$,
  Proposition~\ref{prop:amp}.)
\item $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$: $A\sim\sqrt{x}$,
  so $M=O(\log x)$ and $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ holds
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:A}).
\item $\sigma<\tfrac{1}{2}$: $A\ll\sqrt{x}$, so $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$
  holds trivially, but $T^{(1/2)}$ (temporal cost) is weakened
  as the amplitude falls below the quantum-diffusion threshold.
\end{itemize}
Hence $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$ is the unique value satisfying all four
conditions simultaneously.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\hline
Condition & Amplitude behaviour & Role & Holds for all $\sigma$?\\
\hline
$R^{(1/2)}$ & Prime gaps give $d_\pi\to 0$ & Background & Yes\\
$T^{(1/2)}$ & Prime density gives $d_\pi>0$ in mean & Background & Yes\\
ND & Littlewood sign changes & Background & Yes\\
$E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ & $|\varepsilon|\sim x^\sigma/\log x$; need $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$ & \textbf{Selecting} & \textbf{No}\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Perspective shift.}
Classical methods ask \emph{where} the zeros are.
The present framework asks \emph{what prime universe can exist}:
the three background conditions establish a causal structure for any~$\sigma$,
while $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ selects $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$ as
the unique value at which the prime universe satisfies
quantum causal realizability.

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Abstract foundation: $E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$}\label{sec:decomp}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The four prime conditions of Section~1 are not arbitrary choices.
They arise from a structural decomposition of the classical
quadratic Assumption~$E$ \cite{Li2026R} that reveals its
two independent components.
This section establishes that decomposition and derives
the Quantum Realizability Theorem, explaining \emph{why}
these conditions form the natural framework.
It is logically independent of Sections~\ref{sec:dirA}--\ref{sec:unique}
and may be read separately or skipped on first reading.

\subsection{Classical realizability (background)}

We recall the framework of \cite{Li2026R}.
Work in $U\subset\mathbb{R}^2$ with chart $(t,r)$.

\begin{definition}[Classical assumptions R, E, T \cite{Li2026R}]
\begin{align*}
&\text{(R)}\quad
  \exists\;\delta x_n\to 0,\;v_t>0,\;v_r\ne 0:\quad
  d(x;\delta x_n)/\|\delta x_n\|\to 0,\quad
  \delta x_n/\|\delta x_n\|\to v.\\
&\text{(E)}\quad
  d^2=\bigl(Q(\delta x)\bigr)_++o(\|\delta x\|^2),
  \quad Q\text{ a quadratic form}.\\
&\text{(T)}\quad d\bigl(x;(\Delta t,0)\bigr)\ge c_t\Delta t>0.
\end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Li 2026 \cite{Li2026R}]
Under R, E, T: $Q=dt^2-c_{\max}^{-2}dr^2$ (Lorentzian).
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Decomposition $E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$}

The five-step proof of Li~2026 uses $E$ as follows:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\hline
Step & Used from $E$ & Replaceable by\\
\hline
1--3 & scale $h^2$, continuity & $E'$ \\
4 & $\det Q\ne 0$ (algebraic) & ND (topological)\\
5 & $Q$ is quadratic & $E$ only (Sylvester)\\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Step~4 is the key: the proof uses $\det Q\ne 0$ to exclude degeneracy,
but only the topological property (null set has no interior) is needed.
This gives the decomposition:

\begin{corollary}[$E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$]
Assumption~$E$ is equivalent to $E'$ (scale condition)
plus $\mathrm{ND}$ (topological condition) plus Sylvester
(intrinsic to quadratic forms).
Informally: $E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{definition}[Quantum conditions]
\begin{align*}
E'&:\; d^2=\bigl(Q'(\delta x)\bigr)_++o(\|\delta x\|),
  \quad Q'\text{ continuous, positively 1-homogeneous}.\\
\mathrm{ND}&:\; \mathcal{N}(Q'):=\{v\in S^1:Q'(v)=0\}\text{ has no interior in }S^1.\\
T^{(1/2)}&:\; \lim_{\Delta t\to 0}d^2/\Delta t=c_t>0.\\
R^{(1/2)}&:\; d(x;\delta x_n)/\|\delta x_n\|^{1/2}\to 0
  \text{ for some }v_n\to v,\;v_t>0,\;v_r\ne 0;\\
&\phantom{:\;}\text{and }\exists\,u\in S^1,\;u_t>0,\;Q'(u)<0.
\end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Quantum Realizability]\label{thm:QR}
Under $R^{(1/2)}$, $E'$, $T^{(1/2)}$, $\mathrm{ND}$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item $\exists\,w^*\in S^1,\;w^*_t>0,\;Q'(w^*)=0$ \emph{(null direction)};
\item $\mathcal{C}^-(Q')\ne\emptyset$ \emph{(causal interior non-empty)};
\item $\mathcal{N}(Q')$ has no interior \emph{(sharp boundary, by ND)}.
\end{enumerate}
The result is a Finsler causal structure, not necessarily Lorentzian
(Step~5/Sylvester does not apply to 1-homogeneous $Q'$).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Step~1.} By $R^{(1/2)}$ and $E'$:
$(d/\|\delta x_n\|^{1/2})^2=(Q'(v_n))_++o(1)\to 0$,
so $Q'(v)\le 0$.

\textbf{Step~2.} By $E'$ and $T^{(1/2)}$:
$d(\cdot;(\Delta t,0))^2/\Delta t\to Q'(e_t)=c_t>0$.

\textbf{Step~3.} The path $\gamma(s)$ from $e_t$ ($Q'>0$) to $v$
($Q'\le 0$) is continuous in $S^1\cap\{u_t>0\}$.
IVT gives $w^*=\gamma(s^*)$ with $Q'(w^*)=0$ and $w^*_t>0$,
$w^*_r\ne 0$.

\textbf{Step~4.} By $R^{(1/2)}$ (second part), $\exists\,u$ with
$Q'(u)<0$, so $\mathcal{C}^-\ne\emptyset$.
ND ensures $\mathcal{N}(Q')$ has no interior arc.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Relation to prime conditions]
The prime cost function $d_\pi$ does not satisfy $E'$ literally
(since $\varepsilon$ is a step function, not smooth).
The four prime conditions
$R^{(1/2)},T^{(1/2)},\mathrm{ND},E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$
are the natural analogues of
$R^{(1/2)},T^{(1/2)},\mathrm{ND},E'$
in the number-theoretic setting.
The decomposition $E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$ explains \emph{why}
these are the right conditions to impose; it does not provide
a logical deduction of the prime results.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[H\"older exponents]
Log-log regression on $N=20{,}000$ samples:
a harmonic-oscillator path has H\"older exponent
$\hat\alpha=0.9999\approx 1$ (Assumption~$E$, scale $h^2$);
a Wiener path has $\hat\alpha=0.4999\approx 1/2$
(Assumption~$E'$, scale $h$).
The counterexample $Q'_{\mathrm{deg}}=\max(v_t,0)$ satisfies
$R^{(1/2)},T^{(1/2)}$ but has null set $\{v_t\le 0\}\cap S^1$
(a semicircle, open arc), so ND fails and $\mathcal{C}^-=\emptyset$.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}
%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We have established, with the prime cost function
$d_\pi(x;h)=|\varepsilon(x+h)-\varepsilon(x)|/\sqrt{xh}$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Direction A (rigorous):}
  RH $\Rightarrow$ $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$,
  i.e.\ the prime universe satisfies quantum realizability.

\item \textbf{Direction B (conditional):}
  Any finite set of off-line zeros ($\mathrm{Re}(\rho)>\tfrac{1}{2}$)
  forces $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ to fail (Theorem~\ref{thm:B},
  triangle inequality + Koch for verified zeros).
  Hence $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}\Rightarrow\mathrm{RH}$
  under the conditional premise.

\item \textbf{Remaining gap:}
  Infinitely many zeros in $\tfrac{1}{2}<\sigma<\tfrac{7}{12}$;
  requires Lindel\"of or GUE-level Dirichlet polynomial control.

\item \textbf{Uniqueness:}
  $\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$ is the unique value satisfying all four
  conditions simultaneously (Theorem~\ref{thm:unique}).

\item \textbf{Abstract foundation:}
  The decomposition $E=E'+\mathrm{ND}$ (Section~\ref{sec:decomp})
  shows why the four conditions are geometrically natural:
  $E'$ is the quantum scale condition and ND is the
  topological light-cone sharpness condition, acting
  on separate steps of the causal structure proof.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
The main conceptual contribution is the perspective shift:
instead of asking \emph{where} the zeros are,
we ask \emph{what prime universe can exist}.
The answer is that $E^{(1/2)}_{\mathrm{mean}}$ uniquely selects
$\sigma=\tfrac{1}{2}$, while the three background conditions
$R^{(1/2)},T^{(1/2)},\mathrm{ND}$ establish the causal framework
for any $\sigma$.
